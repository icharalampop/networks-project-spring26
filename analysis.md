Question 1: London had the highest inefficiency ratio at 3.10, just above the 3.0 high-inefficiency threshold. This is somewhat surprising given that London is the closest city in the dataset (5,263 km) and is one of the best-connected cities in the world. The high ratio is likely explained by the fact that even though the physical distance is short, the packet still has to traverse multiple network hops across the Atlantic, go through peering points and internet exchanges, and return, all of which add processing and queuing delay that compounds on top of the already short propagation time. Because the theoretical minimum for London is so low (52.6 ms), even moderate real-world overhead pushes the ratio above 3.0. Interestingly, Lagos, Nigeria (8,255 km away) had a much lower ratio of 1.53, meaning its routing is proportionally more efficient despite the greater distance.

Question 2: Sydney, Australia achieved a ratio of 1.07, the closest to the theoretical minimum in this dataset (Singapore was 1.08). Sydney is 16,229 km away with a theoretical RTT of 162.3 ms and a measured RTT of 173.6 ms, only about 11 ms above the physical limit. This tells us that routing infrastructure between the US East Coast and Australia is extremely well developed. Direct high-capacity submarine cables like the Southern Cross Cable span the Pacific, meaning packets travel a relatively direct path with minimal detours through intermediate hubs. The result is near-optimal routing with very little added overhead beyond the unavoidable speed-of-light delay.

This is confirmed by our data: London (5,263 km, ratio 3.10) and Frankfurt (5,896 km, ratio 2.94) both showed high latency, and Lagos (8,255 km) still only measured 126.6 ms  consistent with a path that goes Boston to Atlantic to Europe to West Africa rather than a direct transatlantic route to Africa.The reason for this is historical and economic. Submarine cable investment has traditionally followed commercial traffic patterns. Europe built extensive cable infrastructure connecting it to Africa's west coast early on (cables like SAT-3/WASC and ACE), while direct North America to Africa cables were not prioritized. As a result, African internet traffic is funneled through European internet exchange points (IXPs) like DE-CIX in Frankfurt and LINX in London before reaching its final destination. According to submarinecablemap.com, Lagos is now served by 7 submarine cables: 2Africa, Africa Coast to Europe (ACE), Equiano, Glo-1, MainOne, NCSCS, SAT-3/WASC, and WACS. While this is a significant number of cables, most of them run along the African coast or connect northward to Europe, not directly westward to North America. To fix this, direct submarine cables connecting West Africa to the US East Coast would need to be built. This would eliminate the European detour and could significantly reduce latency for African users accessing North American services.
