Question 1: Sendai, Japan (Tohoku University) had the highest inefficiency ratio at 15.07, meaning the measured RTT was over 15 times the theoretical minimum. Several other university servers also showed extremely high ratios: Berlin (13.45x), Johannesburg (10.55x), Santiago (9.53x), and Canberra (9.89x).
This is in sharp contrast to the Google targets for the same regions, Tokyo (Google) had a ratio of just 1.62, while Sendai (Tohoku University) had 15.07. The difference is not about geography but about infrastructure. Google operates a massive global network with edge servers, private submarine cables, and dedicated peering agreements that get packets to their destination as fast as physically possible. University servers like Tohoku have none of that, they rely on standard commercial ISPs, shared infrastructure, and multiple routing hops through regional and national networks before reaching the destination.
According to submarinecablemap.com, Lagos, Nigeria is served by 7 submarine cables: 2Africa, Africa Coast to Europe (ACE), Equiano, Glo-1, MainOne, NCSCS, SAT-3/WASC, and WACS. Despite this, Johannesburg's university server had a ratio of 10.55x, showing that having cables present doesn't guarantee efficient routing, internal African network infrastructure and routing policies still add significant overhead.

Question 3: Traffic from Burlington, MA to Lagos, Nigeria almost certainly travels through Europe before reaching its destination. This is supported by our data, London (Google) measured 167.7 ms with a ratio of 3.19, and Johannesburg (Wits University) measured 1,335.5 ms with a ratio of 10.55. The packet to Lagos likely goes Boston to Atlantic to Europe to West Africa rather than taking a direct transatlantic route to Africa.
The reason is historical and economic. Submarine cable investment has traditionally followed commercial traffic patterns. Europe built extensive cable infrastructure connecting to Africa's west coast early on, while direct North America-to-Africa cables were not prioritized. As a result, African internet traffic is funneled through European internet exchange points (IXPs) like DE-CIX in Frankfurt and LINX in London before reaching its destination.
Lagos is now served by 7 cables (2Africa, ACE, Equiano, Glo-1, MainOne, NCSCS, SAT-3/WASC, and WACS), but most of them run along the African coast or connect northward to Europe, not directly westward to North America. To fix this, direct submarine cables connecting West Africa to the US East Coast would need to be built, eliminating the European detour and significantly reducing latency for African users accessing North American content.
